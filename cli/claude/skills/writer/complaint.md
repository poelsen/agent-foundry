# Formal complaint

Register: firm, factual, civil. Not angry, not sarcastic, not threatening. A calm complaint with specifics is far more effective than an indignant one, this is the genre where "add soul" is dialled back hardest, so tell humanizer to cap the personality injection at hand-off.

## The four blocks

1. **Facts**: chronological, with dates, amounts, order and reference numbers, and names. No adjectives. Let the sequence of verifiable facts do the work.
2. **Impact**: one or two sentences on the material effect on you (cost, time lost, service denied). Concrete, not emotional.
3. **Remedy**: one specific outcome the recipient can actually deliver: a refund of a stated amount, a repair by a date, a corrected record. Ask for one clear thing.
4. **Deadline and escalation route**: a reasonable deadline ("please respond within 14 days") and the concrete next step if they don't: the relevant ombudsman, ADR body, trading-standards authority, or association complaints board. Name the real route.

## Rules that keep it effective

- **Do not threaten to sue.** Naming a lawsuit early reads as bluster and hardens the other side; a named ombudsman or ADR route is credible and costs them more to ignore. (This matches consumer-protection guidance.)
- **No shouting**: no capitals for emphasis, no exclamation marks, no sarcasm.
- **One sentence of controlled frustration is allowed** if warranted, then pivot straight back to facts. "I'm disappointed in how this was handled", then the dates.
- **If you cite a legal right, state it once, plainly, and flag the user to verify it before sending.** The model can't confirm a jurisdiction-specific right; don't assert one as settled fact.
