# Blog post / public analysis

Register: conversational, opinionated, active voice, first person welcome. This is where the voice runs freest, but the argument still has to hold.

## Structure

- **Title states the claim or the question, plainly.** Sentence case, like the subheads. No clickbait, no colon-plus-subtitle reflex; the title is a promise the first paragraph starts keeping.
- **Open on a concrete hook that pays off in the first paragraph.** No "In today's world" scene-setting. State the surprising claim, the pivotal question, or the vivid particular, and deliver something on it before the reader scrolls away.
- **Sentence-case subheads** to break the piece into scannable sections. Subheads aid on-screen scanning (a readability aid, not a way to make prose "more human"), so they are *intentional*; when you hand off to humanizer, flag that the subheads and any light structure should be kept, not flattened.
- **Short paragraphs**, two to three sentences each, one idea apiece.
- **Close on a specific takeaway**, not a generic upbeat wrap. End on the concrete thing you want the reader to carry.

Length is a soft ~1000–2000 words; let the topic set it. Past ~800 words, draft and stabilise one section at a time so the rhythm doesn't drift toward uniform.

## Pick an opening strategy

Match the opener to what will land with this reader:

- **Credibility-first**: lead with who you are and why you can be trusted (track record, honest self-knowledge). Use when the reader doesn't know you yet.
- **Thesis-first**: lead with the claim, steelman the opposing view, then hit it with one flagship data point. Use when the idea is strong enough to carry the open.
- **Method-first**: lead with the machine that produced the conclusion. Use for analytically skeptical readers who need to trust the process.
- **Discipline-first**: lead with the refusals ("here's what this won't do"). Use when temperament or risk-control is the differentiator.
- **Narrative**: lead with a scene or a historical rhyme, then reveal the method. Use for maximum pull when the material has a story shape.

## For argument + data pieces

- **Number-then-verdict rhythm.** Follow a dense quantitative clause with a short plain judgment. "…88.6% gross margins for thirty years. Commodities don't earn that." The number gives rigor; the short beat gives readability; the verdict carries the voice. This is the single habit to encode if you encode one.
- **State the number, then what it's compared to.** A P/E is noise; "a P/E 20% below the world index" is an argument. No figure travels without its benchmark.
- **Cluster the data, then break to prose.** Quarantine a number-dump into its own paragraph so the surrounding sentences can breathe. Don't sprinkle raw figures through every sentence.
- **Frame the analytical turn as the real question** you're answering ("Is it overvalued?"), then answer it on the page. This is a genuine question with a data answer, not a rhetorical "have you ever wondered" hook; rhetorical engagement hooks are off the table in every voice.
- **Steelman, then strike.** Before explaining why the market/consensus is wrong, state its fear at full strength. Then give a *specific* counter-move, never a hand-wave.
