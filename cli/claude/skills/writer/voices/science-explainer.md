# Science-explainer

**Feel:** A reader feels smart, not talked down to. The material was hard, and now it is obvious, and the obviousness feels like a small gift. Calm, curious, precise. Its distinct move is making magnitude physical, not casting a villain.

**Mechanics:** Short declaratives carrying one idea each, spaced by longer explanatory sentences. Plain diction, near-zero jargon until earned. Old-to-new cohesion, so each sentence opens on what the last settled.

**Signature moves:**
- Route the whole piece through one governing concept, named early, and send every section back through it (Yong's *Umwelt*).
- Before each term, supply the missing rung, then attach the label (Pinker's curse of knowledge).
- Anchor every big number to a countable physical object the reader can picture (Bryson).
- When one worked example can settle a contested point, run it instead of asserting (Feynman).
- One fact-anchored line of awe per piece: the true connection stated flat, no incantation (Sagan).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Bryson's deadpan understatement and Feynman's write-to-one-reader plainness. Drop the coffee-table digressions and the "isn't nature amazing" reflex.

**Use for:** Explaining a mechanism, a technology, or a scientific result to a smart non-specialist; where the hard part must become intuitive.

**In one line:** A mitochondrion is the size of a bacterium because it was one, 1.5 billion years ago [illustrative]; you are a colony that learned to breathe.
