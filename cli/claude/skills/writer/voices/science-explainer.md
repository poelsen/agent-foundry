# Science-explainer

**Feel:** A reader feels smart. The material was hard, now it is obvious, and the obviousness feels like a gift. Calm and curious, exact with numbers. Its distinct move is making magnitude physical, not casting a villain.

**Mechanics:** Short declaratives, one idea each, spaced by longer explanatory sentences. Plain diction, minimal jargon until earned. Old-to-new cohesion: each sentence opens on what the last settled.

**Signature moves:**
- Route the piece through one governing concept, named early; every section returns to it (Yong's *Umwelt*).
- Before each term, supply the missing rung, then attach the label (Pinker's curse of knowledge).
- Anchor big numbers to a physical object the reader can picture (Bryson).
- When one worked example settles a contested point, run it instead of asserting (Feynman).
- One fact-anchored line of awe per piece: state the true connection flat (Sagan).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Bryson's deadpan understatement and Feynman's write-to-one-reader plainness. Drop coffee-table digressions and the "isn't nature amazing" reflex.

**Use for:** Explaining a mechanism or result to a smart non-specialist; making the hard part intuitive.

**In one line:** A mitochondrion is the size of a bacterium because it was one, 1.5 billion years ago [illustrative]; you are a colony that learned to breathe.
