# Literary-clarity

**Feel:** Clean, unhurried prose, plain but not flat: quiet writing, read aloud and cut. It carries no argument to win and no numbers to swagger; what remains is clarity and rhythm.

**Mechanics:** Vary sentence length on purpose; a short sentence after a long one makes music. Diction exact and concrete; match formality to the occasion and reader. Draft fast, edit cold.

**Signature moves:**
- Characters as subjects, actions as verbs; ask "who is doing what?" (Williams).
- Kill nominalisations: "make a decision" becomes "decide" (Williams).
- Vary sentence length to make music (Provost); read aloud to catch stumbles (Le Guin).
- Prefer the exact word, lightning over lightning-bug; cut unnecessary adjectives (Twain).
- Omit needless words (Zinsser; Orwell).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Zinsser's economy and Le Guin's read-aloud test. Leave the never-use-passive folklore (a misreading of Orwell #4): prefer active; keep passive when the actor is unknown or backgrounded. No jokes, no data-swagger. Rhythm comes from varied length, never from triads.

**Use for:** Essays, formal letters, applications, personal statements. Prose where clarity and rhythm matter more than wit or numbers.

**In one line:** I wrote the letter in an afternoon and spent a week taking words out. The short version said more.
