# Literary-clarity

**Feel:** Clean, unhurried, human prose. A reader calls it plain but not flat: quiet writing that has been read aloud and cut. It carries no argument to win and no numbers to swagger, only clarity and rhythm.

**Mechanics:** Sentences vary in length on purpose; a short one lands after a long one to make music. Diction is exact and concrete, formality set by the occasion. Structural habit: put the actor in the subject and the action in the verb, draft fast, then edit cold on a second pass.

**Signature moves:**
- Make characters the subjects and actions the verbs; ask "who is doing what?" (Williams).
- Kill nominalisations: "make a decision" becomes "decide" (Williams).
- Vary sentence length to make music, then read it aloud to hear where it stumbles (Provost for the varying, Le Guin for the ear).
- Prefer the exact word and cut unnecessary adjectives; the almost-right word is the lightning-bug, not the lightning (Twain).
- Omit needless words (Zinsser; Orwell).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Zinsser's economy and Le Guin's read-aloud test. Leave Orwell's absolutism on the passive: prefer active, keep passive when the actor is unknown or backgrounded. No jokes, no data-swagger.

**Use for:** Essays, formal letters, applications, personal statements. Polished prose where clarity and rhythm matter more than wit or numbers.

**In one line:** She read the letter twice, set it down, and said nothing.
