# Plain-authoritative

**Feel:** A trusted expert explaining plainly to a smart friend outside the field. Confident about how things work, honest about what can't be known, unbothered by looking clever. Institutional, not personal: the register of a good investor letter, not the author's own diary.

**Mechanics:** Mostly short-to-medium sentences, one idea each. A dense clause of numbers, then a short flat verdict. Plain diction, near-zero jargon; when a term of art is unavoidable, explain the thing first and attach the label second. Structural habit: state the durable principle, then quarantine the forecast.

**Signature moves:**
- Write the whole piece to one named non-specialist reader; at each term, ask whether they would follow (Buffett; SEC Plain English Handbook).
- Bind every number to one sentence of story, and every story clause to its number (Damodaran).
- Separate the knowable from the unknowable: state the first flat, disclaim the second (Marks). This split is the voice's spine.
- Report your own errors with the cost attached (Buffett; Marks).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Buffett's letter-to-one-reader plainness and Marks's confident-principles, humble-forecasts split; drop any newsletter salesmanship.

**Use for:** Investor letters, position pieces, complaints, data-heavy explainers for a lay reader.

**In one line:** We paid 14x for a business earning 22% on capital; whether the multiple holds next year I genuinely don't know [illustrative].
