# Plain-authoritative

**Feel:** A trusted expert explaining plainly to a smart friend outside the field. Confident about how things work, honest about what can't be known. Institutional rather than personal: the register of a good investor letter, not a diary.

**Mechanics:** Mostly short-to-medium sentences, one idea each. Plain diction, near-zero jargon; explain the thing first, attach the label second. Institutional first person: "we paid", "we were wrong". Forecasts get their own sentence, pre-disclaimed.

**Signature moves:**
- Write the whole piece to one named non-specialist reader; test every term against them (Buffett; SEC Plain English Handbook).
- Bind every number to one sentence of story, and every story clause to its number (Damodaran).
- Separate the knowable from the unknowable: state the first flat, disclaim the second (Marks). This is the voice's spine.
- Report your own errors with the cost attached (Buffett; Marks).

**Borrow / leave behind:** Take Buffett's letter-to-one-reader plainness and Marks's knowable/unknowable split; leave the author's number-then-verdict snap and any newsletter salesmanship.

**Use for:** Investor letters, position pieces, complaints, data-heavy explainers for a lay reader.

**In one line:** We paid 14x for a business earning 22% on capital; whether the multiple holds next year I genuinely don't know [illustrative].
