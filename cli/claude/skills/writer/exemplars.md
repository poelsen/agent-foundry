# Exemplar techniques, the craft canon

A library of transferable moves mined from writers held in high regard for rigorous, readable, witty prose, plus value-investing bloggers and fiction stylists. Every quote is verified against a real source. Reach in here when a draft needs more rigor, flow, or wit than the genre file alone supplies. Take the technique; don't imitate anyone's surface style.

The governing filter for wit, across every writer here: **if a joke can be deleted without losing information or vividness, it's charm-for-charm, cut it.** Wit must do explanatory work.

## Pillar A, argument + data (rigor)

- **Bind every number to a story, and every story to a number** *(Damodaran)*: for each figure, state the one sentence of story it encodes; for each story clause, name the number it moves. A number with no story, or a story with no number, is decoration. *"A good valuation is a marriage between stories and numbers."*
- **Possible / plausible / probable** *(Damodaran)*: grade each claim: merely possible, actually plausible, or genuinely probable? Discount the merely-possible before it inflates the conclusion.
- **Steelman, then strike** *(Marks; Alexander)*: state the opposing view so well its holders would endorse your version, then answer it. Marks's structural form: "What does the market believe, and why might it be wrong?" (Steelmanning was popularised, not invented, by Alexander; roots in Rapoport/Dennett.)
- **Report your own errors with the number attached** *(Buffett; Marks; Zweig)*: name what went wrong and quantify the cost. A quantified admission reads as rigor and buys credibility for everything else.
- **Notice the missing denominator** *(Harford)*: before trusting a statistic, name what it is *not* measuring: the overlooked definition, the denominator, the missing context. The highest-value rigor move for a data-first writer.
- **Let the demonstration carry the point** *(Feynman)*: when a contested claim can be settled by one cheap worked example, chart, or backtest, run it instead of asserting. (Feynman's ice-water O-ring demo.)
- **Separate the knowable from the unknowable, and disclaim the latter** *(Marks)*: state what's durable and knowable (cycles, risk, behaviour); openly disclaim what isn't (macro forecasts). Confidence about principles plus humility about prediction reads as rigor.
- **Anchor a contrarian claim in the mechanism, then state it flat** *(Zweig)*: back the counterintuitive rule with why it works (regression to the mean, cost drag), then deliver it as a plain imperative, not a hedge.
- **One governing concept** *(Yong)*: find the single idea that makes a sprawling subject cohere, name it early, route every section through it. (Yong's *Umwelt*.) Present synthesised conclusions in your own sentences; don't quote-dump sources.
- **Diagnose by breakage** *(Harford)*: explain how a system works by narrating how it failed; the failure supplies both the stakes and the mechanism.

## Pillar B, readability

- **Write to one named non-expert reader** *(Buffett; Feynman)*: name a specific intelligent-but-non-specialist reader and write the whole piece as a letter to them; at each term of art, ask "would they get this?" Buffett: *"I pretend that I'm talking to my sisters… They will understand plain English, but jargon may puzzle them."*
- **Familiar before unfamiliar** *(Buffett; Lewis)*: introduce the everyday idea first, spell it out once, then attach the label. Explain the concept, then "this is what accountants call GAAP", never the reverse.
- **Characters as subjects, actions as verbs** *(Williams)*: the deepest single clarity mechanic. Ask "who is doing what?" and put the actor in the subject, the action in the verb. "The committee's rejection was based on…" → "The committee rejected it because…"
- **Kill nominalisations** *(Williams; Pinker)*: turn zombie nouns back into verbs. "Conduct an investigation of" → "investigate"; "make a decision" → "decide."
- **Put the money word last** *(Williams)*: end the sentence on the number or verdict you most want to land, not on a trailing qualifier.
- **Old-to-new cohesion** *(Williams)*: open each sentence with something the last one established; end on the new point. This is what makes prose flow without "Furthermore."
- **Defeat the curse of knowledge** *(Pinker)*: assume the reader lacks the context now in your head; supply the missing rung before each term or step. Pinker: the curse of knowledge is "the single best explanation… why good people write bad prose."
- **Anchor every big number to something physical** *(Bryson)*: attach a magnitude to a countable object the reader can picture, so it's felt, not skimmed. Ideal for a numbers-sacred voice.
- **Build understanding as a staircase** *(Urban; Feynman)*: order claims so each rests fully on what precedes; never forward-reference; state the ladder up front. (Take Urban's structure; leave the length and all-caps.)
- **Open a curiosity gap, not a definition** *(Harford; Thompson; Graham)*: reveal a gap in the reader's knowledge first, a concrete puzzle or a pivotal question, so they feel the itch the data will scratch.
- **Omit needless words; concrete over abstract** *(Zinsser; Orwell)*: cut any word doing no new work; prefer the short, specific, concrete statement. "A period of unfavourable weather set in" → "It rained every day for a week."
- **Active by default, passive by exception** *(Federal Plain Language; the honest reading of Orwell #4)*: default active so it's clear who does what; reserve passive for when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately backgrounded (normal in scientific method sections).
- **Draft, then edit as separate passes** *(Lamott; King; Graham)*: write a fast rough draft to find what you think, then cut coldly. King's target: second draft = first draft minus 10%.

## Pillar C, earned wit

- **Deadpan understatement to dramatise enormity** *(Bryson; Roach)*: state something staggering in flat, plain language; the gap between calm tone and huge fact is the effect. The single most transferable wit move for a dry, no-marketing voice.
- **The running joke as a mnemonic** *(Levine)*: one named recurring bit that compresses a real structural truth ("everything is securities fraud"), reused so understanding accumulates. Ration the density.
- **Deadpan definition to puncture jargon** *(Zweig)*: when a term obscures, restate what it really means in one flat sentence. Aim at claims and mechanisms, never at people.
- **Microhumor, not jokes** *(Alexander)*: embed dry understatement or a subverted expectation inside otherwise serious sentences: "the tiniest ghost of a smile," frequent enough to keep the reader moving, subtle enough not to jar.
- **Let the subject be the joke, never the author** *(Roach)*: derive humour from the true weirdness of the material, not your own cleverness. If the joke is about you being witty rather than the thing being strange, cut it.
- **The reframe that reveals a true connection** *(Sagan)*: find the true, non-sentimental fact that makes the mundane astonishing, state it flatly, let it detonate. Cap at roughly one per document; keep the fact-anchor, drop the incantation.
- **Coin a durable concept handle** *(Alexander; Thompson; Sivers; Newport)*: give a recurring idea a short, plain, mechanism-describing name so the reader can carry it and you can reference it cheaply. ("Aggregation Theory," "Deep Work.")
- **Quarantine the delightful tangent** *(Roach; Levine)*: park a good-but-tangential detail in a parenthetical or footnote so the main line stays tight while the curious reader is still rewarded.

## Flow and rhythm (fiction craft, transferable to plain nonfiction)

- **Vary sentence length to make music** *(Gary Provost, 1985)*: the self-demonstrating passage: "This sentence has five words. Here are five more words… I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings." A three-word sentence after a thirty-word one hits hard.
- **Read it aloud; punctuation is breath** *(Le Guin, Steering the Craft)*: "the sound of the language is where it all begins." Where you stumble, the sentence is mis-built. Place a full stop where you want a number to sink in.
- **Prefer the exact word** *(Twain)*: "the difference between the almost right word and the right word… is the difference between the lightning-bug and the lightning."
- **Kill unnecessary adjectives** *(Twain)*: "When you catch an adjective, kill it… then the rest will be valuable." Perfect for a marketing-adjective-allergic voice.
- **Concrete detail carries the abstraction** *(Chekhov, real letter)*: "you'll have a moonlit night if you write that on the mill dam a piece of glass from a broken bottle glittered like a bright little star." The specific number *is* the glint of light.
- **Wit lands at the end of the sentence** *(E.B. White)*: load the sentence so the surprising word arrives last.
- **The unexpected concrete noun** *(Vonnegut)*: deflating specificity is where dry wit lives: not "investors behaved irrationally" but "investors bid a bankrupt company up 400% because it shared a ticker with a video-game shop."
- **Genuine caring beats verbal games** *(Vonnegut, "How to Write with Style")*: "It is this genuine caring, and not your games with language, which will be… most compelling." Simplicity is the delivery system for depth, not the enemy of it.

## Value-blog patterns (from blogs the author admires)

- **Value and Opportunity (memyselfandi007)**: compress who/what/how-big/at-what-price into the opening thesis sentence; convert a valuation gap into an annualised return benchmarked against the risk-free rate; be willing to end a piece with "I'll pass"; name your losers in reviews ("EVS, Wise… *once again*"). The wit is just honesty.
- **The Brooklyn Investor**: open by naming the consensus you're about to push against, then plant a calm, specific disagreement; pin every "expensive/cheap" to a number and a historical comparison (28× P/E vs 1987's ~100% overvaluation); explain dynamics as a plain feedback loop; self-deprecation to build trust, not to hedge.
- **Thorleif Jackson (Nordnet, the namesake)**: open with a dated personal milestone ("It's now 3½ years since…"); teach the method with an everyday analogy before any metric ("look at the grades and pick the ones that did best"); state the rule as an ordered two-step filter; report underperformance flatly, allow one human word of frustration, then diagnose it; exit a loser by naming the exact metric that invalidated the thesis. The transparent-teacher register.

## Myths and misattributions, never encode these as rules

- **"If you can't explain it simply you don't understand it" is NOT Feynman or Einstein**: unsourced folklore. Feynman's real, humbler inverse: sometimes failure to simplify means *the experts* don't yet understand it.
- **"Never use the passive voice" is a myth**: a dogmatic misreading of Orwell #4 and Strunk & White (who themselves misidentified the passive). Prefer active; use passive on purpose.
- **Strunk & White is not a grammar authority**: good on economy, unreliable on grammar (Pullum, 2009). Use it for concision only.
- **"Write at the Nth-grade level" is a myth of inference**: a low readability grade measures surface plainness, not target audience or shallow content. Chase clarity; the score is a byproduct.
- **"Kill your darlings" is Arthur Quiller-Couch (1913-14)**, not Faulkner/Twain/Wilde/King. **"A shorter letter if I'd had more time" is Pascal**, not Twain. **"Write drunk, edit sober" is not Hemingway** (nearest source: Peter De Vries, a novel, ironic). **"I vary the sentence length, and I create music" is Gary Provost**, not Scott Alexander.
- **Don't encode zombie rules**: never split an infinitive, never open with "and"/"but," never end on a preposition, absolutist which/that. Mainstream linguistics treats these as superstitions.
- **Sourcing hygiene**: famous maxims are a magnet for orphan quotes (Twain/Hemingway/Orwell/Wilde absorb them). Verify before citing, or omit. Treat illustrative figures (Housel's "$300B Vanderbilts," Lewis's "50 people") as illustrations, not hard statistics.

If the author wants the primary sources: **Buffett's preface to the SEC's *A Plain English Handbook* (1998)**: the whole "write to one named non-expert" discipline in a few pages; and **Pinker, *The Sense of Style***: why expert prose fails, and the safest wholesale adoption for this voice.
